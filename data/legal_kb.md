# CareGap Legal Knowledge Base
## U.S. Healthcare Billing Law & Patient Rights Reference

---

## 1. No Surprises Act (Effective January 1, 2022)

### What It Covers
- **Emergency services**: Patients cannot be balance-billed for emergency care at any facility, in-network or out-of-network.
- **Non-emergency services at in-network facilities**: If an out-of-network provider treats you at an in-network facility (e.g., an out-of-network anesthesiologist during a scheduled surgery), you are protected from surprise bills.
- **Air ambulance services**: Out-of-network air ambulance providers cannot balance-bill beyond in-network cost-sharing amounts.

### Patient Protections
- You only owe your **in-network cost-sharing amount** (copay, coinsurance, deductible).
- Out-of-network providers and facilities must provide a **Good Faith Estimate (GFE)** of expected charges for scheduled services.
- Uninsured or self-pay patients can request a GFE; if the final bill exceeds the estimate by $400 or more, you can dispute it through the **Patient-Provider Dispute Resolution (PPDR)** process.

### Good Faith Estimate (GFE) Rules
- Providers must give a GFE within **1 business day** if the service is scheduled within 3 days.
- Providers must give a GFE within **3 business days** if the service is scheduled 3-10 days in advance.
- The GFE must include: expected charges, diagnosis codes (ICD-10), service/procedure codes (CPT/HCPCS), facility fees, and any other anticipated items.

### Dispute Process
1. Contact the provider's billing department first.
2. If unresolved, initiate a dispute through the **CMS PPDR portal** within 120 days of receiving the bill.
3. An independent dispute resolution (IDR) entity reviews the case and issues a binding decision within 30 days.

## 2. Right to an Itemized Bill

### Federal Protections
- Under the **No Surprises Act**, uninsured and self-pay patients have the right to receive a **Good Faith Estimate** and an itemized bill.
- Medicare patients have an explicit right to an itemized statement under **42 CFR § 405.

### What an Itemized Bill Must Include
- Date of service
- CPT/HCPCS procedure codes with plain-language descriptions
- Quantity of each service
- Charge per unit
- Provider name and NPI number
- Facility name and address
- Diagnosis codes (ICD-10)
- Total charges and adjustments

### How to Request
- Call the provider's billing department.
- Say: *"I am requesting an itemized bill with CPT codes and individual charges for each service rendered."*
- Providers must furnish this within **30 days** of the request.

## 3. Balance Billing Protections

### What Is Balance Billing?
Balance billing occurs when an out-of-network provider bills you for the difference between their charge and what your insurance paid. For example, if a provider charges $500, insurance pays $300, and the provider bills you $200 — that $200 is a "balance bill."

### When Balance Billing Is Prohibited
- **Emergency services** (any facility, any provider) — No Surprises Act
- **Out-of-network providers at in-network facilities** (without patient consent) — No Surprises Act
- **Medicare patients** — Providers who accept Medicare assignment cannot balance bill
- **Medicaid patients** — Federal law prohibits balance billing in most cases

### When Balance Billing Is Allowed
- **Elective, non-emergency out-of-network services** where the patient was informed in advance
- The provider must give written notice and obtain **consent at least 72 hours before** the service
- The consent form must include the estimated out-of-network charges

## 4. Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA)

### Applicability to Medical Debt
- Third-party debt collectors must follow FDCPA rules when collecting medical debt.
- As of **July 2022**, the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) no longer include **paid medical debt** on credit reports.
- Medical debt under **$500** is excluded from credit reports (effective April 2023).
- Medical debt cannot appear on credit reports until **365 days** after being sent to collections (previously 180 days).

### Patient Rights Under FDCPA
- Right to request **debt validation** within 30 days of first contact.
- Collectors cannot call before 8 AM or after 9 PM.
- Collectors cannot use abusive, deceptive, or unfair practices.
- You can send a written **cease and desist** letter to stop collection calls.

### Debt Validation Letter Template
```
[Your Name]
[Your Address]
[Date]

[Collection Agency Name]
[Collection Agency Address]

RE: Account Number [XXXX]

Dear Sir/Madam,

Pursuant to the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (15 USC § 1692g), I am
requesting validation of the debt referenced above. Please provide:

1. The name and address of the original creditor
2. The amount of the debt, including an itemized breakdown
3. Proof that you are licensed to collect debt in my state
4. A copy of the original signed agreement or contract
5. An itemized statement of the medical services with CPT codes

Until this debt is validated, please cease all collection activity.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
```

## 5. Common Billing Errors to Check

### Upcoding
- Billing for a **more expensive procedure** than what was performed.
- Example: Billing CPT 99215 (complex office visit, ~$250) when CPT 99213 (low-complexity visit, ~$110) was provided.
- **Red flag**: Charges seem disproportionate to the service received.

### Unbundling
- Charging separately for procedures that should be **billed together** at a lower bundled rate.
- Example: Billing each lab test individually instead of using a panel code (e.g., CPT 80053 for a comprehensive metabolic panel).
- **Red flag**: Multiple line items for related tests or procedures.

### Duplicate Billing
- Charging **twice** for the same service.
- **Red flag**: Same CPT code appearing on multiple lines for the same date of service.

### Phantom Charges
- Billing for services, supplies, or procedures that were **never provided**.
- **Red flag**: Line items you don't recognize or recall receiving.

### Incorrect Patient Information
- Wrong diagnosis code (ICD-10) leading to **claim denial** and patient liability.
- **Red flag**: Diagnosis doesn't match the reason for your visit.

## 6. How to Dispute a Medical Bill — Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Get Your Itemized Bill
- Request an itemized bill with CPT codes (see Section 2).

### Step 2: Compare Against Fair Pricing
- Use CareGap's CMS PFS pricing tool to compare each CPT code's charge against the Medicare-allowed amount.
- A charge significantly above the Medicare rate (e.g., 3-5x or more) may be excessive.

### Step 3: Check Your EOB
- Review your **Explanation of Benefits (EOB)** from your insurer.
- Compare what the provider charged, what insurance paid, and what you owe.

### Step 4: Contact the Billing Department
- Call the provider and reference specific line items.
- Say: *"I've reviewed my itemized bill and CPT code [XXXXX] appears to be [upcoded/duplicated/higher than the fair market rate]. Can you review this charge?"*

### Step 5: File a Formal Dispute
If the billing department doesn't resolve it:
- **Insured patients**: File a grievance with your insurance company.
- **Uninsured patients**: Use the CMS Patient-Provider Dispute Resolution process.
- **All patients**: File a complaint with your **State Attorney General** or **State Insurance Commissioner**.

### Step 6: Dispute Letter Template
```
[Your Name]
[Your Address]
[Date]

[Provider/Hospital Name]
Billing Department
[Provider Address]

RE: Account/Invoice Number [XXXX]
    Date of Service: [MM/DD/YYYY]
    Patient: [Your Name]

Dear Billing Department,

I am writing to formally dispute the following charges on my medical bill:

1. CPT [XXXXX] — [Description] — Billed: $[XX.XX]
   Issue: [Describe the issue — e.g., "This charge is 4x the Medicare-allowed
   rate of $XX.XX" or "This service was not performed" or "This appears to be
   an upcoded charge"]

2. CPT [XXXXX] — [Description] — Billed: $[XX.XX]
   Issue: [Describe the issue]

I request that you review these charges, correct any errors, and provide an
adjusted bill within 30 days. Please respond in writing.

If this matter is not resolved, I will file complaints with [State Attorney
General / State Insurance Commissioner / CMS].

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Phone Number]
[Email]
```

## 7. State-Level Protections (Selected States)

### California
- **AB 1611**: Limits out-of-network emergency charges to in-network rates.
- Patients can dispute bills through the **DMHC Independent Medical Review**.

### New York
- **Emergency Surprise Bill Law**: Patients held harmless for out-of-network emergency bills since 2015.
- Independent Dispute Resolution for provider-insurer payment disputes.

### Texas
- **SB 1264**: Out-of-network balance billing protections for emergency and certain non-emergency services.
- Patients only owe in-network cost-sharing.

### Florida
- **HB 221**: Balance billing protections for emergency services and surprise bills at in-network facilities.

### New Jersey
- **Out-of-Network Consumer Protection Act**: Among the strongest state protections; limits patient liability for inadvertent out-of-network care.

## 9. Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **CPT Code** | Current Procedural Terminology — 5-digit code identifying a medical service |
| **ICD-10** | International Classification of Diseases — diagnosis code |
| **HCPCS** | Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System — codes for supplies, equipment |
| **EOB** | Explanation of Benefits — insurer's summary of what was billed and paid |
| **NPI** | National Provider Identifier — 10-digit provider ID number |
| **Balance Bill** | Billing the patient for the difference between provider charge and insurance payment |
| **GFE** | Good Faith Estimate — required cost estimate before scheduled services |
| **IDR** | Independent Dispute Resolution — binding arbitration for billing disputes |
| **PFS** | Physician Fee Schedule — CMS's schedule of Medicare payment rates |
| **Allowed Amount** | Maximum amount an insurer will pay for a covered service |
| **UCR** | Usual, Customary, and Reasonable — benchmark for fair pricing |
